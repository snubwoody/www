Zambia has had loadshedding for over a decade as of 2026. This all has to do with its main source of electricity, which is hydroelectricity. Hydroelectric power makes up [more than 85%](https://www.moe.gov.zm/?page_id=2198) of the power in Zambia, and for the past decade it has not been able to keep up with the demand of the population.

## History of load shedding

First we need to ask why there is load shedding in the first place and where it all started. A large amount of energy demand comes from the mining sector. In 1975 mining accounted for more than 60% of total energy consumption. Following the nationalisation of the mines in the 70s and falling world copper prices, copper production fell for the next few decades. This left Z.E.S.C.O. with an excess energy supply from the 1970s to the early 2000s. Z.E.S.C.O. knew that this surplus would not last forever, so they started warning the government in the 1990s, requesting more capacity be built. However, started public consultations were held, there was resistance due to the fact that electricity prices would need to be increased. So little to no energy plants were built during this period.

In the late 2000s copper prices and production started rising back to their original levels. This eventually led to load shedding in 2008 when the mining sector, combined with the rest of country, had more energy demand than Z.E.S.C.O. could provide. However, this did not have too much of an impact on Z.E.S.C.O.'s operations, since it's operating costs were still being covered, so no excess capacity was built. It is in 2016 that things started to get more extreme, due to the severe drought faced by southern Africa. In addition to the existing excess demand, there was now a severely reduced production capacity because of lower water levels. Since then there has been a large energy deficit, in addition to Z.E.S.C.O. failing to keep up with its operating costs.

## Energy production

Zambia's energy is primarily produced from hydroelectricity, however, it can't just keep on building more dams. There's only so many water sources, and fewer that would produce enough energy to be sustainable. Plus, hydroelectric dams take decades to build. The quick and easy solution would be to build more coal power plants; Zambia has consumed less than 1% of its total coal reserves[^2]. However, in more recent times, there has been a massive push against non-renewable energy[^1], for good reason. Which makes it hard because many of the world's countries used coal for cheap energy production when they were developing, but now Zambia must look for alternative options.

Most of the current investment has been going into solar power. Zambia, and Africa in general, has incredible potential for solar energy. In addition to the fact that solar panel prices have been dropping over the last few decades, this would provide a cheap and efficient alternative source of energy.

Zambia has untapped [Uranium deposits](https://world-nuclear.org/information-library/country-profiles/others/uranium-in-africa#zambia). Nuclear energy has gotten a bad reputation because of certain historical events, but nuclear energy is extremely safe and clean. After the Uranium has been used for nuclear fission, it can be recycled or stored safely in the ground.

There are plenty of other sources of energy, but if Zambia continues to depend solely on hydroelectricity then it will have load shedding for many decades to come.

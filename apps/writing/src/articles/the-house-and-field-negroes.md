title: The house and field negroes
preview: false
published: 2026-01-14
---

The house negro detests his field brothers and sisters, by being in the house there is some inherent superiority 
embedded in his psyche. He is closer to the master and as such he is 'higher' in the ranks, which fills him with a 
sense of pride. No longer working in the field, he spends his days calmer, even getting to sit at the same table as 
the master.

The field negro looks up to his house brothers and sisters, "they are half free" he says in his mind. The dream of 
most field negroes is to end up in the house, there is better shelter, food and freedom. Sometimes house negroes get 
to ride out in the car with the masters. Some field negroes would rather be in the house than be free. One thing that 
they both seem to have forgotten is that a slave is a slave.

Occasionally there arises a house negro that pities his field brethren, there is fundamentally little difference 
between them, he feels some guilt for his position. He wants to help free all the slaves, but how can he? He doesn't 
have guns or whips or chains. He doesn't know how the engine machine operates. He doesn't even know the roads more 
than 2 Km out of the field. Furthermore, his brothers and sisters are so beaten, bruised, berated and broken that 
freedom seldom crosses their minds. "A house negro till I die". However, some people, house and field alike, know that 
if they all get together, they hold the real power. Negros out number their masters 10 to 1, being physical workers, 
they are much faster and stronger too. But their minds have been broken, and that is the greatest trick the master 
pulled. By using the term 'master' and 'worker' a mental separation was placed, growing over the years.

In fact the physical work is the easiest, it would merely take one afternoon. The real work, tedious and lengthy, is 
to be done in the mind. Convincing the negroes that they could actually be free is the greatest form of liberation 
one could provide.
